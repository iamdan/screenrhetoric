About the Video

This piece argues for the value of affect in communication. Linking affect to media is a core aspect of screen rhetoric. Visuals, sounds, and mixed materials evoking emotions are fixtures of screen rhetoric. Sean Morey tells us, "certainly print can evoke emotions, feeling, and mood, but not in the same way as visual or tactile forms of delivery" (107). Morey shows the ways that modes can vary as means of delivery for emotions. Dance or war imagery, for instance, provide channels for "sense-data that appears in many digital modes, producing a mood, an emotion, but not always making this conscious to the viewer" (53)

In the production of text, then, the "original" discourse and design . . . are not pristine layers (or strata) that undergird production layers. Instead, these layers are all changed; the text is changed. It is no longer the origianl act of meaning-making: it is an entirerly new one -- Non-Discursive Rhetoric 159

Taking up affect and emotions in multimedia makes sense. The challenge is pausing to consider how such a move relates to print. If, as Jody Murray suggests, digtial media can become "the vehicle[s] from which belief becomes lived experience" and that "images that are ambiguous, that defy translation into words, are precisley the kind of images that are themselves the most generative, the most non-discursive," then channels like academic prose must have correlary strengths or weaknesses. We should add to insights about the rich imagistic possibilities of media reminders about the circuits and channels available through words.

To deliver in this way is to perform the electronic monument, through which the deliverer becomes attuned to the information sphere until she notices a sting, theorized as Roland Barthes’s punctum, a sensation under the skin. . . . The deliverer is not looking to solve the problem, but to understand her relationship to the problem and how that problem manifests itself inside of her – Rhetorical Delivery and Digital Technologies 254

Images move us, and Morey astutely points out the connections with agency that come from this moving, calling on W.J.T Mitchel to point out that "images have both lives and desires" (145). The posthuman impulses surfaces again as visuals take over our familiar habits of composing. If we allow them to. The transactional nature of these emotional exchanges of meaning
