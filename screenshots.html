This video is recording of a performance that brings together several video clips hosted by YouTube, a web browser window with a discussion forum posting, an audio recording of the reading of that discussion forum posting, and two windows containing a video with a layered montage of painted surfing imagery and other screen elements. This version was recorded on February 8th, 2012 and posted to vimeo.com.

Use of Materials

Jason Loan's "Tooling with Ambient Response" http://vimeo.com/35816842 extends the boundaries of this video with its connection to earlier projects. Uploaded to vimeo three days (Jan 28th, 2012) before the initial experiments with Screenshots, the piece itself is a crystalization of several months of video exchange between Loan and the author. Two days prior (January 26th) the author had uploaded a video titled "Spontaneous Overflow (Spillover)" https://vimeo.com/35730075 to vimeo. That Spillover video is a capturing of materials leftover from the process of creating the video, Waves. It also includes a video compilation of quotations from creativity researcher, Keith Sawyer. "Tooling with Ambient Response" is Loan's reworking of the spillover video. Loan has added elements to the piece, including a web broadcast by poet Steve Roggenbuck, painted surfing imagery

The discussion forum posting by Michael Salvo is also a core element of the video. Acting as a respondent to HASTAC's publication of a collection of materails on sound, Salvo experiments with the form of the academic response, weaving sound into the prose via song lyrics and striking a tone that might be cast as cynical, irritated, resistant. This posting has been read aloud by the video author, and then incorporated into the performance as an audio file. The rest of the materials in the piece are videos played directly from the hosting site, YouTube. "Butthole Surfers - Pepper," "Widescreen vs. Pan & Scan," and "Davy Crockett, King of the Wild Frontier - Part 1" were all played directly from the web during the live performance of the piece.

This video was made by recording the screen and bringing media materials together through a live performance. The materials include multiple browser and media windows, so that the final compilation includes lots of edges and adjustments as windows are arranged. The occasion for the piece was created in part by encountering the video clip, Widescreen vs. Pan & Scan, in which Sydney Pollack warns against reformating films to four-by-three formats. Concurrently, a piece called Feel the Noise: Sound, Music, & Technology was published on the HASTAC web site. Michael Salvo, as a respondent to the piece, composed the text that is used in the video. On the evening of January 31st, 2012 and the afternoon and evening of Febuary 8th, 2012, multiple improvisation and testing mixes were performed. The performance of February 8th recorded at 9:09PM is the final capture.

In Ambient Rhetoric, Thomas Rickert points out that "the boundaries between brain and body, self and world, language and thought, beginning and end, are permable" (100). Like Gregory Ulmer today and Plato long ago, Rickert offers space and a sense of place through the concept of chora as a frame for ambient composing. Place in this light is not a call for "presence" but a contextual means of engaging the material world. "It includes the background by means of which things show up as what they are" (55) The boundaries are permeable, so the "background recedes, or withdraws, even as it generates." This sense of place is evokes displacement as composers mix with materials among these emerging spaces. For Rickert, it calls for rhetorics not fully commensurate with familiar modes of representation. Following Derrida, Rickert notes that the concept "disrupts representation itself (and hence rhetoric, too, if it is conceived in terms of sybolicity) even while it remains fundamental for the passage of representation" (55).

Boundaries can't be easily fixed as spatial or choric knowledge has a "performative dimension . . . that replaces (or, better, reworks) the fixed places of topic invention" (71) In Screenshots, the video Widescreen vs. Pan & Scan features Sydney Pollock noting the differing compositional strategies a director might deploy depending on screen format. A wide format, for instance, shifts aspects of film time, as the director "can tell much more story, faster with all of that space to use." Pollock seeks to regain control of film information by giving directors authority to make decisions about format. Ambient approaches, however, suggest that regardless of the size of the canvas, the composer articulates a message by engaging (through performance) a world in which boundries constantly shift so that "disclosure is itself manifold and ambient; it can be performed, practiced, and activated in many ways, only so much of which will be directly appropriable to consciousness, intention, or theorization" (72).

Erin Manning and Brian Massumi take up the relationship between framing and invention as they articulate an approach to scholarship developed around research-creation. Noting that "emergent process, dedicated to the singular occurrence of the new, agitates inventively in an open field. Programmed organization, on the other hand, functions predictably in a bounded frame and lends itself to reproduction." Moving toward research-creation requires paradigms "of conditioning, rather than framing. The difference is that conditioning consists in bringing co-causes into interaction" (93). Manning expands on this bringing together as she discusses the concept of the machinic, noting that "to render something 'machinic' is to inscribe it in a process that couples it with the environment out of which it emerges" (Relationscapes 87).

Switching to "conditioning, rather than framing" offers a renewed mode of understanding something like screen format for composers. Manning and Massumi point out that, even during interactions between research-creators and their environments, "enabling constraints" are needed. "An enabling constraint is positive in its dynamic effect, even though it may be limiting in its form/force narrowly considered" (93). The frame becomes less a means of keeping information in or out and more a material resistance that enables creators to productively work with materials. In this way, working with aspect ratios might be seen as technique that shape (and are shaped by) the movements between screen composers and their environments. "Techniques are not descriptive devices—-they are springboards. They are not framing devices—-they activate a practice from within. They set in motion" (x).
