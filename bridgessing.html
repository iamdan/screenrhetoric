Bridges Sing

This video was originally performed at the Conference on College Composition and Communication in 2017. It combines cell phone video with videos taken from the internet and videos of student data projects. These materials are mixed as shifting layers with varying opacities in the video. It also includes music and sound effects. The transcript below captures the narration and major textual and media elements.

00:00 Opening title reads: Tweet Analysis, Ambiguity, and Storytelling. A cursor appears on screen and adds text to the title that reads: A Dialog. Below the title appears the text: Daniel Anderson iamdan@unc.edu. The cursor adds @iamdan. The cursor highlights and then deletes all of the text on the title screen, and then begins typing. A computer-generated voice reads aloud the text being typed: See there is this thing called writing. And it’s great.

00:30 The narrator’s voice responds: I love writing. But I’m supposed to be here to talk about data. Writing. . . . ah, I mean data is so cool. Look at how it helps us make sense of something like an election. On screen web articles discussing the 2016 election and data appear. Okay. Maybe I should rethink that. The 2016 presidential election was about the death of data, and the rise of stories.

00:52 The computer-generated voice speaks: It's not that politics don't matter, but writing, now that's a human activity.

01:06 The narrator’s voice responds: I don’t know. Death is so serious. At most, maybe data just had a bad day. And if the point is to tell stories and write our lives, data is doing that even as we speak. And it’s kind of already political. And it’s kind of taken on a life of its own. Here it is built into an assignment. And here is someone using it for scholarship in the humanities, telling stories with it.

01:37. On screen an image of a web site with a class assignment appears. The narrator’s voice continues: And it’s really kind of interesting to play around with. We used this spreadsheet. 06:05 On screen the camera continues to move over bridges and the river. The narrator continues speaking: I once wrote some poems about the organic mechanic. 06:48 The computer-generated voice and the narrator speak in unison: Amen to that, my friend, amen. On screen, the camera flies over a bridge on which two light-rail trains are crossing. The video ends.

Use of Materials

This piece brings together a few sources of several different kinds. A news item opens up the piece. The item provides a few visuals that evoke the 2016 Presidential election, and then brings to light concerns related to data, particularly by highlighting the phrase, "it was the day the data died." screenshot of a scholarly article related to qualitative analysis of Twitter data is also included. The piece also makes use of several images that depict spreadsheets that have been populated with tweet-related data using the TAGS (Twitter Archiving Google Spreadsheet) tool. These spreadsheets have been developed around a number of data-oriented class activities.

There are two video clips that have been excerpted to provide some of the visuals in the piece. Initially, a home video depicting figures on a train and bridges around Portland Oregon is layered among the materials to emphasize human and material connections. In the latter half of the video, excerpts from "Portland, Oregon - Bridges from a Drone" play a prominent role in the closing visuals. These are layered among other materials, and an excerpt from a video by the band Explosions in the Sky is brought into the soundtrack. Opaque images depicting downtown portland in Google Maps are also included.

The piece also inlcudes excerpts from two student projects. The projects were created in a class called Digital Literature. Students were asked to conduct a number of data-oriented activities and then to produce a media-based "presentation that responds to the prompt: how are culture, community, and identity mediated by technology?" Project by Adam Engel and Nete Biere have been excerpted.

Finally, the video contains materials created by the author. A number of images and videos have been recorded using a cell phone camera and are included here. In addition, images and excerpts from the publication, "I'm a Map: I'm a Green Tree" appear near the end of the piece.

About the Video

This video was origninally created as part of a panel presentation on data in composition studies at the 2017 Conference on College Composition and Communication. This was a particularly challenging presentation, as many at the time were experiencing a sense of data shock after the 2016 US Presedentional election. At the time, questions about the weight to give data in our interpretations and conclusions were pressing. And the relationsip between data and storytelling seemed to be opening up.

Instead of waging the humans vs. robots war yet again, couldn't we ask a different set of ethical questions: How does one judge and evaluate the conflicting and competing narratives spun by machines and humans? Ethical Programs 60

The video directly evokes posthuman concerns as it concretizes a human-machine dynamic through dialog. The conversation links human and machine through the text-to-speech processing and the computer-generated voice. The voice takes on the despondence associated with the "death of data" stories in the news. The human narrator tries to console the machine by pointing out their collaborations around data. James Brown Jr. suggests that these kinds of overlapping activities emerge as algorithms act as rhetorical devices and as humans develop "machinic thinking" (139). Even the approaches or heuristics humans develop, from Brown's perspective, can be seen as "machines that enact ethical programs and solve problems" (139). This blurring extends to the relationship between data and narrative, as "the interpretation of data will always require narrative" (138) and "narrative and data are not separable" (136).

At around 01:26 in the video, the chime of a text message arriving can be heard in the audio track of the piece. The chime alerts us to the live-writing aesthetic of the video. The piece was composed by ripping snippets of material quickly from online videos, capturing segments of typing on screen, and creating cell phone footage. With these materials, computer-speech and sound effects were mixed to create a video without the voice of the narrtor, which was added live at the presentation (and has been recorded and added here). The many layers of materials contain the kinds of ephemera we might expect to find in notes or draft materials develped prior to delivering an academic research presentation.

The more profound challenge we face is to accept the ambiguity of knowledge, the fundamentally interpreted condition on which data is constructed. Joanna Drucker

The dialog between the narrator and data is linked to networks and machines. At 02:24, the connection disrupts the dialog in the form of an unexpected train horn. The conversation veers toward the personal as the narrator describes the emotional tenor of a train horn. The surprise of the machine indicates the experience of the train horn is new. Here, posthumanism fully emerges as we reach the logical conclusion of extending agency to non-human actors. In this move, as Ian Bogost sums it up, "things constantly machinate within themselves and mesh with one another, acting and reacting to properties and states while still keeping something secret" (27). Graham Harman describes these interactions as “interconnected, [but] always through indirect or vicarious means” (192). The "properties and states" of the freight train are linked with the moment and place of hearing and with the history of human industry, and they recede even as they emerge in the sounding of the horn, which is itself ambient and flowing with human affect. It's no wonder the computer has trouble taking it in.

This emotional reasoning returns directly in the closing segments of the poem. In addition to the narrator evoking parental stories, the visuals and narration take up a series of poems created by the author. The poems explore the beauty of machinic connections and the ways humans project emotion and capture stories through machines. Much like the machinations or material tunings that posthumanists deploy, Matthew Crawford offers a persona. register for such projections, "a third thing, which is a kind of ethical involvement. [The mechanic] looks for clues and causes only if he cares about the motor, in a personal way" (95). The poems celebrating this involvement were hidden in the code of an earlier publication, but one of them is brought to the surface here:

The organic mechanic
sets his ring tone,
choosing bird chirps:
the peep in the poplar or,
sometime later,
in the inking evening
the distant owl
yopping its wahoo.
