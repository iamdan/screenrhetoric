Truing

The video is a screen recording of a live performance that brought together and manipulated several media items. Three video windows with performances of the song, "Holocene," by Bon Iver appear. An iTunes window also plays an audio recording of the song. Additional videos with images of bubbles and information about bubbles and images of bicycle wheels appear. There are also several Sticky Note windows in which the cursor types during the performance.

00:00 The video opens with multiple windows on screen. A video hosted on YouTube in the upper-left shows a male figure with a guitar. In the upper-right, a video shows a live performance of the song, "Holocene," by Bon Iver. In the lower-right a video with a female figure holding a guitar appears. In the lower-left, we see a video clip showing an old man on a beach blowing enormous bubbles using two sticks and some string. The cursor selects play on all of the materials. The cursor moves to the top of the screen and brings forward an iTunes window containing an audio file of the live performance of the song, "Holocene," by Bon Iver. The cursor selects play, and then a visualizer is turned on, translating the iTunes window into a computer-generated image of horizons, planets, and suns.

00:25. The figures in the YouTube videos begin speaking, offering instruction on how to play the song: “uh, I’m going to try and explain how to play Bon Iver’s. . . .” ; “Hello, YouTube! Um. . . .” The vocals in the live performance of the song begin. The cursor moves to the right of the screen and brings forward a sticky note window and begins to type: “We’re here to make an idea real, a movie. It will be a moving thing that brings response. The bee sting. The bicycle. Guitar string.”

01:17 The cursor brings forward a video clip in the lower-left of the screen and selects play. An image of a spinning bicycle wheel in a truing frame appears. The cursor moves back to the sticky note and continues typing: “The shearsman strumming into being the bread and the stone, which links us to craft, which leads us to art, which brings us to now.”

02:02 The cursor minimizes the sticky note and then brings a new sticky note on screen and continues typing: “We nee[d] words like water. We need their circulations for turning ideas, for truing them and bringing them into tune, the way a wheel might speak through its spokes.”

02:40 In the bottom-left, the video of the man blowing large bubbles is replaced by the video of the spinning bicycle wheel. For a moment, the sticky note and its words are obscured, then it returns to the foreground and the typing continues: ”Thus, we, too, give voice through the wire.

02:56 The cursor brings the video of the man making giant bubbles forward; the turning wheel is no longer visible. The typing continues: We’re shifting like sand or sea drifting over plates, like wetlines changing place with the tides. We’re lifting. Collecting. Spacing. And repeating. Gathering before coming ashore.

03:42 The live performance of the song concludes its second verse and moves into a soft instrumental segment. The cursor minimizes the second sticky note and moves it off the screen. The cursor switches the positions of the videos with male and female figures demonstrating how to play the song; the male voice can be heard giving instructions: “on the sixth string, the third string, and the second string. . . .” The woman’s voice is also briefly audible: “you just kind of have to feel it out.” The live performance of the song is stopped. The cursor brings forward the iTunes window and selects stop; without the music, both guitar instructors’ voices are more audible, but their words overlap and are difficult to discern.

04:08 As the videos of guitar instruction and of a man truing a bicycle wheel continue to play, the cursor brings a video describing the science of bubbles to the foreground and selects play. The narrator in the science of bubbles video speaks: “There is a method to the bubble madness that unlocks some pretty basic laws of nature. Law number one, they act like a prism, reflecting the basic colors of the universe.” The woman teaching the Bon Iver song begins singing softly in the background. An interview voice in the science of bubbles video speaks: “White light coming in is actually composed of all the colors in the rainbow and based on the thickness of the film, all the different colors refract off in different ways, so just like a prism, we get the whole spectrum in every bubble. The amazing thing to me is that when you look up close it looks like bubbles are clear, but there is so much color going on.” A second interview voice responds: "That is correct. It's every color of the rainbow. I like to think that the colors are a map, showing you that the film is thick in some places and thinner in others.” The science of bubbles video is muted and the male guitarist can be heard: “he’s playing a lot of the same notes, but . . . sometimes the length which some of the notes are held are different. . . .”

O5:03. The cursor rearranges videos on screen and then selects play on the live performance of the song. The music—still soft—continues. The cursor brings forward the iTunes window and selects play on the live performance of the song. The song is now layered, echoing due to the timing being slightly off on the two performances. After a few seconds, the cursor turns down the sound on the iTunes window and moves the window off the screen.

05:34 The cursor brings a new sticky note to the screen and begins to type: “Light casts movements like blue or green or blue and green. The tracings are breath or wind or liquid flowings like skin turning bubble and returning wet with mind sight. The mixing laps at the overlaps of image, song, word, or heard whisper from a front porch. Shh. . . .This is important.”

06:44 The live performance of the song concludes, and the cursor presses stop on the video in the upper right. The audio from the guitar instructors is audible; the man says “and, um, after he sings ‘magnificent’ there’s a little pause for dramatic effect.” For a few seconds, both the man and woman sing different parts of the song; their voices do not quite mesh. The video with bubbles and bees moves to the upper-right corner of the screen. The cursor turns up the sound on the science of bubbles video, brings forward another sticky note, and begins to type: ”This is the healing of the split. It’s the organic mechanic opening a window to say, we can change shapes, we can move together as we do in this moment through the film.”

07:15 In the science of bubbles video, the two interview voices converse: “So there it is. It's pretty amazing. You think of it as one event, instant event, but there you really see the tear. You never think of it as a directional event. It just looks like it's there and it's gone.” The narrator of the video speaks: “Watch the bubble burst again, even slower this time. . . .” The male guitar player sings in vague falsetto. The narrator of the science of bubbles video continues, “There's some complex science going on here. Surface tension holds the soap bubble molecules together while the air inside the bubble forces them apart. When the bubble begins to tear, soap molecules shoot out in all directions as tiny droplets.”

08:05 The cursor brings a video on screen containing a male figure in front of a bicycle wheel on a workbench. The cursor selects play and the figure begins plucking the spokes and speaking: “After radially truing the rim, pluck a number of spokes on the drive side and listen for the tone. . .” The ringing of the spokes is barely audible over the speaking of the guitar instructors. The man continues: “Then pluck a number of spokes on the non-drive side and also listen for the tone.” The cursor lowers the sound of the video.

08:40 The cursor increases the volume on the male guitarist video: “the third verse, the Christmas-y verse, you want to slow down a bit and just play long, languid notes and try to think of the suitable timing to hit the next bit of the line.” The video is muted. In another video with the spinning bicycle wheel, the narrator can be heard: “if you hear one that’s kind of like a clunk in there then you’ll know that that one’s extra loose.” The cursor mutes that video, too. The cursor slowly mutes the video of the woman playing and singing the Bon Iver song. The video ends.

The video features four variations of live performances of the song, "Holocene," by Bon Iver. The core element is a recording captured on July 29th, 2011 that has been selected from numerous "concert videos" captured with mobile phones and posted on the YouTube web site. This video is played live in Truing, and a version of the song is also played using an audio player. Two additional versions of the song are also included in the piece. Both of these have been gathered from "how to play" videos hosted on YouTube.

A number of other videos are used to provide visual and sonic materials. These include videos providing instruction on bicycle wheel truing as well as a video on the science of bubbles. All of these materials are excerpted and woven into the larger messaging of the poem.

Truing also includes a compilation video, or bubble track (bubblescrashingandrolling.mp4) that brings together imagery of bees and flowers, waves and shorelines, and figures creating large soap bubbles. These materials have been sampled from numerous items hosted online. The video also includes a narrative track. The volume of the bubblescrashingandrolling video is turned down in the live performance of Truing, so no narration from the bubble track can be heard here; however, the hidden narration sheds light on the themes in the piece.

About the Video

The fundamental tone, and its overtones, have, each of them, their own intensities. This pattern of intensities can be analysed into the relative intensities of the various tones and the absolute intensity which is the total loudness. The scale of relative intensities enters into the final quality of the note, with some independence of its absolute loudness. Alfred North Whitehead, Process and Reality (234)

This video was created as a model project for an undergraduate writing class. The assignment focused on media-oriented composing, but required that videos include significant elements that bring words on screen. The piece was further developed and included with the publication, Watch the Bubble, in 2012. It captures a live performance that includes manipulation of multiple videos, an audio player, and live typing.

A core component of the performance is the mixing of sound. The primary soundtrack is provided by a live performance of the song, "Holocene," by Bon Iver. To create a sense of echoing, a second iteration of the song is woven into the mix. The second iteration instantly creates an opportunity for tuning, as the volume needs to be adjusted to open attention possibilities for other media. Similar adjustments must be made to the volume levels of the "how-to" performances and other media elements. Whitehead notes that "relative intensities" must be weighed as we consider the feelings associated with even a single note. This weighing of intensities is an apt model for screen composing. Meaning emerges as elements accrue overtones and merge into harmonies.

Such composing calls for "analysis [that] reveals an abstract qualitative pattern which is the complex relatedness of the fundamental tone-quality with the tone-qualities of its select overtones" (234). The audio elements in the piece call for this kind of co-emergent, intensity-based, layered, experiential reading. The call is amplified by the additive nature of the compositional method, combining more and more visual and sonic materials as the performance plays out. The two versions of the Bon Iver performance resonate with each other through adjustments in volume and timing. They similarly relate to the "how to" performances. Their relationship here, though is more adjacent, introducing distance and dissonance as well as similarity and resonance. The composing brings the similarities and differences together through tunings of intensities.

A speculative pragmatism takes as its starting point a rigor of experimentation. It is interested in the anarchy at the heart of all process, and is engaged with the techniques that tune the anarchical toward new modes of knowledge and new modes of experience. It is also committed to what escapes the order, and interested in what this excess can do. It implicitly recognizes that knowledge is invented in the escape, in the excess. Erin Manning, The Minor Gesture (38)

The patterns and resonances in the piece derive from adjustments not only in intensity but also in sequencing. Heavy repetition has refined the timing of where to queue the playhead, when to select play on a video clip, and how to arrange the materials in the live performance. In these interactions, the sense of tuning is experienced by the human performer working with (and against) compositional materials (that include words). Andrew Pickering links these interactions to accommodation and resistance between humans and materials as we engage with invention: "Such practice consists in the reciprocal tuning of human and material agency, tuning that can itself reconfigure human intentions" (21). The shaping of human intentions means the text of the performed screen composition is never the same. Each instance differs from others as subtle adjustments emerge through "a goal-oriented practice [that] takes the form of a dance of agency" (21).

Further complicating these material and sonic tunings is their situational context. Whitehead notes that the feelings linked to sonic materials are extended by the "complex ordered environment composed of certain other actual entities which, however vaguely, is felt by reason of this audition. This environment is the datum of this feeling. It is the external world, as grasped systematically in this feeling" (324). Thomas Rickert considers these contexts in terms of "attunement" as he outlines a model of ambient rhetoric. The mixing of audiovisual materials recasts this context to allow the composer to create a sonic world by tuning elements within the performative environment of the computer screen.
