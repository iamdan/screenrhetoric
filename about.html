About the Project

The materials collected here are offered to test the proposition that, as academic work expands into video formats, it can challenge behaviors based on print and open alternative possibilities for scholarship. The tensions in this proposition derive in part from the significant extents to which print publication has shaped our understanding of scholarship. Witness the countless claims for the disruption of new media made in hard-bound books. Print-centric approaches no doubt provide powerful ways of capturing and sharing knowledge. They also shape what counts as understanding. If, as Gregory Ulmer suggests, "print favors and supports or augments an analytical mode of thought" (140), then alternative modes may be opened up as we move beyond words.

Each page opens with a video that represents aspects of video scholarship and screen composing. A short discussion follows with information about the creation of the video and exploration of thematic and rhetorical concerns. The discussion is not meant to replicate the intellectual concerns taken up in the video. Nor is it intended to explain the videos or justify their value. Rather, the text is meant to extend the alt-scholarship impulses of the project. These impulses aim not for closure, but for opening up meaning by adding intellectual layers that create resonances or initiate circuits for more exploration.

Each page also includes a transcript of the video. These transcripts provide another means of engaging the videos through textual descriptions. These descriptions shed light on a range of questions regarding accessibility and video scholarship. The questions can be evoked by comparing a transcript from a more traditional, talking head video with one derived from a screen composition. Providng dialog or narration can be accomplished with captions or a basic transcript that tracks easily with the temporal progression of the video. Compare that with this transcription of four seconds of the audiovisual activity from the video, Waves.

Providing this level of detail, however, reveals the accessibility challenges associated with screen composing. Descriptions of visual and sonic material, on one level, seek to provide access to video content. However, reproducing the experience in a textual mode is not only impractical but also in many ways incommensurate with enabling people with differing abilities to engage the work. The three second video segment transcribed above, for instance, requires one minute and fourty-two seconds to process with a screen reader. Further, editorial decisions increase exponentially as items in the sonic and visual field are identified for inclusion in the transcript, and then translated into textual form. The transcriptions included with the project, seek a balance between providing access to spoken, written, sonic, and visual materials, while recgonizing the challenges of delivering video scholarship in textual registers.

Finally, each page includes information about the materials and sources used in the videos. As with accessibility, our understanding of scholarly uses of materials is recast through video scholarship and screen composing. A number of intellectual property questions can be explored by imagining this use in terms of the multiple factors that go into decisions about the use of materials in multimodal compositions.

The central components of the project are images, sounds, and text gathered from the internet. In many cases, these are excerpted and integrated into the videos in typical scholarly fashion. In some cases, materials are used in their entirety, evoking considerations related to amounts of materials used in a project. In some instances, entire texts are used, but not fully integrated into the composition--as in whole blog postings used to perform machine operations on texts. In other cases, significant portions of materials are used; however, their full presence never appears on screen as windows are stacked, arranged, made transparent, or hidden on screen.

At this point, we need to think through the difference between simply copying and displaying materials and deliberately integrating them into an intellectual project to make a point; the mashing together of entire blog postings in Waves conveys the message of printcentricity; the sounds of the introduction of a live performance in Trainsplaing prepare the ground for the affective arguments that will be offered by speeding trains. The selections of material and their integration among the numerous other artifacts and layers in the videos is performed critically and creatively with the aim of moving conversations about scholarship toward screen composing.

Further, we need to consider the ways in which screen composing is an operation that renders the materials in a new way. In these instances, the new composition is a remix, nothing at all like the originals. Any item within any of the videos makes up a small portion of the whole experience, as the materials that are on screen at any given moment are layered together with browser and media windows, performances, citations, narration, and screen activities that make up the rest of the project. Whatever materials are used are transformed by mixing them with multiple other sources.

In all of the videos, materials are deeply integrated and linked with the messages of the text. Given that the U.S. Copyright office describes "transformative uses [as] those that add something new, with a further purpose or different character, and do not substitute for the original use of the work," the materials woven into the project serve a transformative purpose. They evoke particular points about their given subjects and further the overall message about alternative approaches to scholarly response. Given the key role of materials in furthering critical intellectual conversations and their transformative integration into the videos, the project follows familiar scholarly protocols of quotation with attribution.

The videos collected here range from speculative experiments in writing through affective media to audiovisual expositions of scholarly topics to concrete reports on class activities. Spending time with several of the videos will reveal audiovisual resonances, common screen composing techniques, and thematic connections. The arrangement of the videos in the project menus is not meant to imply a proper viewing order. The collection is meant to be sampled. That said, there is a chronology to the development of the pieces and commonalities among the video. The project highlights concerns with academic knowledge production. Scholarship, Rita Felski notes, is often "a mode of interpretation that adopts a distrustful attitude toward texts." This attitude translates into criticism "with distinct qualities: distance rather than closeness; guardedness rather than openness; aggression rather than submission; superiority rather than reverence" (#). A number of dangers accompany these mind-sets. Foremost is the foregoing of compassion and empathy as scholars compete with one another for intellectual recognition. Many of the videos lay out these concerns, and then offer varied alternative modes of response.

As concerning as the suspicious nature of some scholarship are the ways that familiar behaviors make it difficult to recognize new modes. Erin Manning and Brian Massumi warn that disciplines tend to reject "transversal modes of thought" in favor of "a static organization of preformed categories" (#). Given the poststructural movements of contemporary theories of knowledge, the traditional channels seem an arbitrary and fixed mode for developing and sharing scholarship. Joanna Drucker and Bethany Noviskie offer an alternative in methods that "go beyond descriptive, generative, or predictive approaches to become speculative" (#). Drucker and Noviskie ask us to bring speculative and experimental modes to computing, but the directive can just as easily be applied to scholarship. Many of the videos respond to this call.

Video scholarship also offers insights into familiar conceptual topics questions. Through narration, the integration of citations, and the representations offered by audiovisual materials, the project explores scholarly topics. Models of thinking based on networks or systems are particularly suited for theorizing, as the windows and activities of screen composing have "a deep virtual reach to archives and databases" (Friedman 19). Echoing claims made for hypertext, networked and autopoetic theories are actualized in the circuits of screen composing. Bruno Latour tells us, "go with the flow . . . follow the circulating entities" (237). The videos experiment with emergent materialities and networked modes as they take up scholarly topics.

The concerns of video scholarship also reshape activities in the classroom. The pedagogical approaches promoted in the project bring into sharp relief the printcentric orientations of academic work. Ian Bogost identifies the most significant fixture of traditional scholarship as writing, and asks "must scholarly productivity take written form?" (89). Typcial assignments ask students to "fix meaning and erect solutions" (Davis 252). Inviting students to compose for the screen involves them with practices that foreground extralinguistic meaning, emergence, affect, and ambiguity. Familiar expectations might find students studying works rich with irreconcilable complexity and open questions; however, any hint of such ambiguity in student responses is to be eliminated in any responses students might create. Many of the videos collected here explore the implications of opening composition activities to the movements of screen composing.
