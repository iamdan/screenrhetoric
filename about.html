About the Project

The materials collected here test the proposition that, as academic work expands into video formats, it can challenge behaviors based on print and open alternative possibilities for scholarship. The tensions in this proposition derive in part from the ways that print publication has shaped our sense of scholarship. Witness the countless claims for digital disruption made in hard-bound books. Print-centric approaches provide powerful ways of capturing and sharing knowledge. They also determine what counts as understanding. If, as Gregory Ulmer suggests, "print favors and supports or augments an analytical mode of thought" (140), then alternative modes of thinking may be opened up as we move beyond words.

Each page opens with a video that represents aspects of video scholarship and screen composing. A short discussion follows with information about the creation of the video and exploration of scholarly and rhetorical concerns. The discussion is not meant to replicate the messages offered in the videos. Nor is it intended to explain the videos. Rather, the text extends the alt-scholarship impulses of the project. These impulses aim not for closure, but for opening up meaning by adding intellectual materials that create layers, evoke resonances, initiate circuits, or open pathways for more exploration.

Transcripts

Each page also includes a transcript of the video. These transcripts provide a means of engaging the videos through textual descriptions. These descriptions shed light on a range of questions regarding accessibility and video scholarship. Providing transcripts of monologue, dialog, or narration is relatively straightforward when words are delivered through video. Compare that with this transcription of twenty-five seconds of the audiovisual activity from the video, Waves.

02:08 In the text box, the copied opening paragraph of the NYTimes.com article is visible: “This is a blog. There, I’ve said it. . . .” In the Chrome browser window with the “Welcome to Pine Point" project, the animated bird grasps a piece of yarn in its beak and flies off, dragging new imagery onto the screen, including a scan of a flyer with a line drawing of a figure in a welder’s mask. At the top of the screen is a label that reads: Ads from Pinepoint Yearbooks. In the text editor window, the cursor deletes text until the phrase, "We seek tr" remains. The run button is clicked, and “tr” becomes traditional. The Run button is clicked, and “traditional” becomes transform. The cursor at the end of transform begins typing, and transform become transformation. From the "Welcome to Pinepoint" project, a voice begins to speak: “I don’t think I’ve ever had closure with Pine Point. I always. I always feel sad when I, like you said, when I tell somebody I grew up in Pine Point and they ask me when do I come back. I go, Oh, it’s not there anymore.’ And I do feel a sense of, I don’t know what it is. For me it’s weird to say that I don’t have. . . .”
[02:33]
Providing this level of detail reveals the accessibility challenges associated with screen composing. Can descriptions of visual and sonic material reproduce the experience of the videos in a textual mode? The twenty-five second video segment transcribed above requires one minute and twenty-four seconds to process with a screen reader. Creative decisions increase exponentially as items in the sonic and visual fields are identified for inclusion in the transcript, and then translated into textual form. The transcriptions included with the project seek a balance between providing access to spoken, written, sonic, and visual materials, while recognizing the challenges of delivering video scholarship in textual registers.

Materials and Sources

Each page also includes information about the materials and sources used in the videos. A number of intellectual property questions are recast in terms of media and networked knowledge as materials are deployed in video scholarship and screen composing.

The central components of the project are images, sounds, and texts gathered from the internet. In many cases, these are excerpted and integrated into the videos in typical scholarly fashion. In some cases, materials are used in their entirety, evoking considerations related to amounts of materials used in a project. In some instances, entire texts are used, but not fully integrated into the composition--as in whole blog postings used to perform machine operations on texts. In other cases, significant portions of materials are used; however, their full presence never appears on screen as windows are stacked, arranged, made transparent, or hidden on screen.

The purposes associated with video scholarship also recast questions about using materials. The selections of images, sounds, and texts and their integration among the numerous other artifacts and layers in the videos is performed critically and creatively with the aim of moving conversations about scholarship toward screen composing. These combinations are made to offer criticism, commentary, teaching, and scholarly response. The mashing together of entire blog postings in Waves conveys the message of printcentricity; the sounds of the introduction of a live performance in Trainsplaining prepare the ground for the affective arguments that will be offered by speeding trains. The formats differ, but their deployment for scholarly purposes resonates with familiar academic uses of materials.

In the creation of video scholarship, screen composing also renders materials in new ways. In these instances, the new composition creates remixes in which materials are made to be nothing at all like the originals. Any item within any of the videos makes up a small portion of the whole experience, as the materials that are on screen at any given moment are layered together with browser and media windows, performances, citations, narration, and screen activities. The materials are transformed by deliberately mixing them with multiple other sources. The US Copyright office describes "transformative uses [as] those that add something new, with a further purpose or different character, and do not substitute for the original use of the work." Through recombinations, materials are remade in ways that surpass simple representation.

Affirming, Speculating, Theorizing, and Teaching

The videos collected here range from experiments in screen composing to audiovisual expositions of scholarly topics to concrete reports on class activities. Spending time with several of the videos will reveal audiovisual resonances, common techniques, and thematic connections. The arrangement of the videos in the project menus is not meant to imply a proper viewing order. The collection is meant to be sampled. That said, there is a chronology to the development of the pieces and commonalities among the videos. The arrangements below provide additional details that might be helpful for navigation and one possible way of grouping items.

Affirming

The project highlights concerns with academic knowledge production. Scholarship, Rita Felski notes, is often "a mode of interpretation that adopts a distrustful attitude toward texts." This attitude translates into criticism characterized by "distance rather than closeness; guardedness rather than openness; aggression rather than submission; superiority rather than reverence" (222). A number of dangers accompany these mindsets. Foremost is the loss of compassion and empathy as scholars compete with one another for intellectual recognition. Many of the videos lay out these concerns, and then offer alternative, affirmative modes of response.

Speculating

Familiar behaviors make it difficult to recognize new modes. Erin Manning and Brian Massumi warn that disciplines tend to reject "transversal modes of thought" in favor of "a static organization of preformed categories" (30). Given the poststructural movements of contemporary theories of knowledge, the traditional channels seem an arbitrary and fixed mode for developing and sharing scholarship. Joanna Drucker and Bethany Noviskie offer an alternative in methods that "go beyond descriptive, generative, or predictive approaches to become speculative." Drucker and Noviskie ask us to bring speculative modes to computing, but the directive can just as easily be applied to scholarship. Many of the videos respond to this call.

Theorizing

Video scholarship also offers insights into familiar conceptual questions. Through narration, the integration of citations, and the representations offered by audiovisual materials, the project articulates perspectives on scholarly topics. Models of thinking based on networks or systems are particularly suited for such theorizing, as the windows and activities of screen composing have "a deep virtual reach to archives and databases" (Friedman 19). Echoing claims made for hypertext, networked and autopoetic theories are actualized in the circuits of screen composing. Bruno Latour tells us, "go with the flow . . . follow the circulating entities" (237). The videos experiment with this directive as they take up scholarly topics.

Teaching

Video scholarship reshapes activities in the classroom. The pedagogical approaches promoted in the project bring into sharp relief the printcentric orientations of academic work. Ian Bogost identifies the most significant fixture of traditional scholarship as writing, and asks "must scholarly productivity take written form?" (89). Typical assignments ask students to "fix meaning and erect solutions" (Davis 252). Inviting students to compose for the screen foregrounds extralinguistic meaning, emergence, affect, and ambiguity--qualities traditionally edited out of student work. Many of the videos collected here explore the implications of opening classroom activities to the movements of video scholarship and screen composing.
