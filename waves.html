Waves

This video brings together two screen recordings of performances with videos player and web browser windows. On screen a number of videos play, including a video with a live musical performance and a video that captures a multimedia documentary about an abandonded town in Canada. These materials play while the cursor moves through a series of blog postings in browser windows. The blog postings are copied into a text editing file, and autocomplete and word counting tools are used to manipulate the text.

The central components of the project are the blog postings that are used to compile the text that is manipulated on screen. A total of ten blog postings are included, and the entire text of each posting is used. Another key piece in the video is the Welcome to Pine Point media project. The project is included in all but thirty-six seconds of the piece, but makes up a small portion of the whole experience, as the materials that are on screen at any given moment are layered together with browser and media windows.

Several videos and compilations are also included. A video with a live performance of the song, "Postcard from 1952," is woven into the audio track and plays continuously. Both Pine Point and Postcard from 1952 evoke messages that resonate with the passing of time and a sense of loss. The materials are meant to convey nostalgia in a mode that is more allusive than that of print scholarship.

About the Video

The authoring and reading environments for interpretative scholarly work are only just beginning to be designed in such a way that the linear, finite conventions of print media can be changed for the constellationary, distributed, multi-faceted modes of digital media. Joanna Drucker, "Humanities Approaches to Interface Theory" (2)

On January 9th, 2012, Stanley Fish published an opinion piece in The New York Times that self-consciously identified itself as a blog posting and challenged the transformative potential of the digital humanities. In the days that followed, countless responses were posted on blogs. Waves is a speculative test of what a response to Fish and to those subsequent blog postings might look like in video form. The video was made through a process of live capturing of screen activities (with minor post-capture editing to add a list of sources). This process was used twice, once to capture a base layer that includes the media elements of the video and a second time to capture activities performed with blog postings and a text editor. The video was posted to Vimeo on January 23rd, 2012.

This video features humans and machines performing together. An unseen mouseclick brings a Twitter timeline on screen. A keyboard shortcut transforms the cursor into a spotlight or brings up a dialog box with a word count. These human-computer interactions illuminate co-emergence as software is used to compose and the author and operating system mix. The creation is kinetic and linked with the body. The hand must click to bring forth the window. On screen, layers of hardware and software respond to create meaning. The use of a cursor spotlight focuses attention, as the circle of light moves and creates emphasis. The mouse and machine mark out meaning through a kind of movement. Dwight Conquergood calls for a performance that emphasizes "a decentering agency of movement, intervention, transformation, struggle, and change" (19). The performances in Waves seek these transformations by bringing together humans, networks, and software through activities of representation.

The alphabetic aspects of the response involve moving and counting text. Command-c and command-v, over and over. Quickly copying blog postings feels mechanical until we realize that a point is being made by pasting together all that text. The spotlight highlights the word count as prose accumulates in the document. There are many more possibilities in this space. For years, we've known that digital texts differ in their fluidity, in the ease with which we move and mix them. Their digital nature, Lev Manovich, suggests makes new media texts numerical, modular, and ready for automation (27-32). Digital representation, Wolfgang Ernst, argues "dissolves any semantically meaningful archival unit into discrete blocks of signals" (60). This digital turn offers an "epistemological revolution" (60) for Ernst and yields database logics for Manovich based on "the ability of the computer to produce endless variations" (236). Yet, here, we are simply moving large blocks of academic text.

As long as the keyboards of computers are alphabet based like those of typewriters, the paradigm of printing remains dominant; progressively, though, the mouseclick is replacing the keystroke as the device for directing the monitor and the orientation is shifting to visually perceived information landscapes. Wolfgang Ernst, Digital Memory and the Archive 121

The mixing, counting, and searching in Waves represents what Manovich considers "low-level" implications of the shift in behaviors after digitization. The autocomplete function simply searches the text document for words that match the initial letters being typed and inserts suggestions. Still, this low-level shift is enough to recast interactions between humans and machines. At 05:00, the cursor is placed at the top of the text editing document for improvisational typing. The live typing continues until a breakdown develops [06:00] as the autocomplete function is tried multiple times with the intent of completing the phrase “this is the place where we make scholarly” with the term “waves.” Autocomplete fails to generate the term, and a question is typed [06:09]: “where are the waves?” The phrase “We seek w” is typed, and autocomplete is tried again, this time finding the term “waves,” which has now been added to the document just three words away [06:16]. So much of the composition is driven by digital affordances--from copying to counting to computer-generated layers of images, sounds, and texts. And yet the human hand remains always in play, bringing the cursor back to the document and adding more text for the digital searching routine to process. We find humans and machines performing together in the composing.

Ernst makes a distinction between humans and machines when discussing digital logics, noting that, when comparing words, images, or sounds, "the difference between aesthetic regimes exists only for the human user" (118) as the computer "calculates images, words, and sounds basically indifferently" (118-19). Since these material-level aspects of digitality operate "at a level that is not directly accessible to human senses" (60), the "physical layer below symbolically expressed culture can be registered only by the media themselves" (61), shifting us away from textual analysis and toward reading of artifacts and machines. A reading for Ernst "concentrates on the nondiscursive elements . . . not on speakers but rather on the agency of the machine" (45). Beyond the material level of binary code, however, humans and machines recombine, particularly through experiences of representation. Ernst asks us to contemplate the reading (calculating and capturing) "that only computing can do" (62) but allows that humans and machines will continue to mix through representation that creates a synesthetic convergence. Though digital operations play out "below the sensual thresholds of sight and sound," it's still possible that "synesetheically, we might see a spectographic image of a previously recorded sound memory" (60). Thus, we can recognize the new materiality of digital media with "analysis [that] opens culture to noncultural insights without sacrificing the specific wonders of culture itself" (63).

For screen composing, the levels Ernst describes and the focus on media objects translates into an invitation to play with combinations of machine- and human-driven artifact-textuality. Framing media and digital materiality on their own terms is complicated as representation is added. Ernst notes the need to "[describe] the nondiscursive practices specified in the elements of the technocultural archive without simply reducing the archive to its technical apparatuses" (114-15). In practical terms, software and representation are brought together through composing, since "in multimedia space, sound and images can be shifted, cut, stored, and reloaded, as in word-processing software" (121). As Erin Manning explains, "digital technologies must work at the level of perceptual emergence" (72). Emergence is the key term here, as it brings together "technogenetic" movements that blend the organic and technical. Rather than working "prosthetically, technology must become capable of actively making sense" (72). The sense that is created, like the human-computer interaction in Waves, "interweaves the organic and the technogenetic" (72).
