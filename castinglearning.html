Casting Learning Into Flowing Streams

Transcript

This video was created with a screen recording of a performance on the computer on January 30th, 2011. An additional screen performance was captured on June 11th, 2011 to create an opening frame for the piece. An audio recording of the narration was added after the screen captures were brought together in a video editor. The piece showcases materials from an undergraduate English class called performing literature. The piece also includes videos hosted by YouTube that are played live in the performance, including a live performance of the song, "River Under African Trance" by the band, String Cheese Incident. The materials were curated by the author and three students who also developed screen recordings to reflect on their work. The compilation of materials was published as "Casting Learning into Flowing Streams" in the College Composition and Communication Online 1.1 (2012). A prepublication draft of the project is available at http://teachmix.com/tiwid/

Materials

A major component of the video is made up of three sub-videos that have been compiled by the author. These videos--Shifts in Composing, Performing in the Learning Studio, and Open Assignments, Affective Modes, and Creativity--are themselves made up of excerpts from student portfolios. These portfolios contain reflections related to the project included in the publication. The three videos have been edited to highlight excerpts from these reflections. In the recording of the Casting Learning video, the three sub videos are played and arranged on screen. The excerpts from student reflections are used with permission.

The video also includes three videos that are hosted on YouTube. A live performance of the song, "River Under African Trance," by the band, String Cheese Incident, plays throughout most of the video. A video called "Big Trains Rolling" depicts train imagery. A video called "Pottery Wheel Demonstration" and a video called "The Valley" provide more visuals. These materials are played live during the performance. All of the materials are mixed together to create the new, Casting Learning piece.

The piece also includes narration and the manipulation of an image file, as well as imagery from the published article. These materials have been created by the author and his collaborators.

About the Video

The assignments in this class . . . would not ask students to promote a single thesis, progress through a focused argument, or come to a (necessarily) premature conclusion. . . . In this class, students would be invited to disrupt their own positions, to contradict themselves, to expose all that must be hidden and excluded in the precious name of clarity. -- Breaking up at Totality 242-43

Diane Davis calls for a pedagogy that's transformative, dangerous even. "Writing involves risk," she says. This pedagogy would welcome ambiguity and be linked with authenticity and emergence. This approach disrupts the closure-oriented modes of college, "forcing a writer constantly to double back on herself and her 'texts,' to crack them open again to expose her own assumptions, to reveal inconsistencies, motivations, contradictions, limitations, incantations" (243). Student video compositions and reflections develop (with/from) genres that challenge expectations for scholarly work. Casting Learning is itself an emergent text built through this process, a process begun with open assignments mixed with media.

Reflection is another key mode of this scholarship. Reflection opens spaces for creativity, both in the projects under consideration and in the composition of the reflection. This distance between project and reflection, a layering, opens spaces for composing to emerge and offers authentic engagement with materials. In studies of creativity, Keith Sawyer notes, "when a person knows he or she will be rewarded for the quality of their work, they can't stop thinking about that reward, and they find it impossible to get into the flow state where they're doing the task simply because they like doing it" (53). The videos included in Casting Learning carve out their own conventions for the genres of media reflection. This experimentation opens spaces where new forms and engagements with materials play out. This composing is performative, improvisational, happening "in a zone between complete predictability and being out of control" (253). The student voices in Casting Learning describe the results: "I got into the zone . . . I didn't even notice I'd been sitting there for hours."

How are these alternatives invented? One element of the practice is the manifesto, amounting to a discourse on method--a rhetoric or poetics--for a new genre or mode of production: a program of experimentation. In teaching theory heuretically a seminar tries out some of these vanguard poetics. -- Heuretics xii

Emergent composing in mediated spaces with ambiguity creates the poetics expressed by Gregory Ulmer in his book, Heuretics. Here, a composition is "an experiment: it is offered not as a proof or assertion of truth but as a trial or test" (38) Ulmer cites Julia Kristeva in describing "a space that is neither that of propositional knowledge nor commonsense opinion. This boundary or frontier (interface) calls for invention conducted as a transgression of the rules." We find "passages" between systems of meaning that call into question "classic mimesis, argumentation, judgment, realism" (176). As familiar sense and intelligibility break down, meaning must "be approached indirectly, by extended analogies" (67), an approach that "collapses the distinction between concrete and abstract" (128) until the "boundary or frontier (interface) function of chora on the verge of language is glimpsed through artistic, poetic practices" (176).

This heuretics traces from Plato to Kristeva to Derrida to Ulmer and beyond through discussions of chora. We find a fit between this kind of composing and alternative scholarship as "chorography promises something more than just an alternative to concept formation as a way to organize data into meaningful sets. It promises also to engage the users' premises in the process of learning, opening a mutually transforming circuit between judgment and theory" (202). This circuit is extended as seeing, hearing, reading, thinking, producing, consuming co-emerge through screen composing. We experience these passages and circuits when we perform this composing. The moments link feeling with judgment and evoke insights and the joy of the sublime (Ulmer 143). And they offer a corrective to a logic of print that "favors and supports or augments an analytical mode of thought based on . . . the abstract demands of logic" (140). Instead, screen composing supposes that bringing together media through emergent mixing promotes a mode of thinking in which judgments "have been rendered ambiguous, uncertain, undecidable" (214).
