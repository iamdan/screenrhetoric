Blessing Critique

This video brings together a number of quotations and narration to discuss the subject of alternative scholarship. It also shows a performative response to a poem. The performative response contains several layers with images and live music. The transcript below captures the narration and performed response and key texts on screen.

00:00 Title screen: The Blessing of Rescuing Critique Daniel Anderson @iamdan. The video opens with the text of a quote on the screen (See aside). Imagery of wild horses appears in the lower portion of the video. The narrator speaks: In this piece, I show how multimedia invites us to move from concrete to abstract, opening avenues for emergent interpretation. I also experiment with a mode of response that recognizes some of the limits of critique and closure. Critique can be competitive, exclusionary, banal. Closure can lead us to overlook the generosity of the world around us, including the world of the texts we study.

[00:37] The text of the poem, "A Blessing" begins scrolling on the screen. The narrator speaks: Practicing a mode of alt-scholarship, I created a response to this poem scrolling on the screen, "A Blessing," by James Wright. The poem is set off a rural highway near Rochester, Minnesota. It is about two companions who stumble across a pair of horses in a meadow. The poem suggests a bond between human and animal and among humans, an emotional connection.

[01:04] The narrator continues speaking: This emotional connection is often lost among the logic of academic closure. But multimodal is well suite to convey affect. Can it also help us produce critical analyis that resists closure? The works we study are rich and ambiguous, connecting with all of our senses, unfolding like flowers. Why not our responses?

[01:28] The narrator continues: These kinds of responses can create new subjectivities. "A Blessing" depicts a coming together as the speaker and his companion connect with "two Indian ponies." And the poem extends that human-animal connection with a metaphor that transcends the subject/object divide and generates a new sense of self at one with the world. "Suddenly I realize / That if I stepped out of my body I would break / Into blossom."

[02:00] On screen a folder displays a number of videos with timestamps from December 17, 2015. The narrator speaks: On a Thursday morning, I worked up a response that plays with layering, cameras, and screens.

[02:08] Imagery of wild horses appears on screen. The narrator continues: I started with a YouTube clip that depicts horses. I began bringing together verbal and visual representations; the literal associations translated into mostly corresponding moving images. I then used my laptop camera and Photobooth to film an adaptation.

[02:22] On screen a red guitar held in someone's lap fills the screen. A hand begins strumming the guitar.

Use of Materials

Many of the items included in the opening segment are standard citations incorporated as images into the video. These raise few issues about intellectual property but several questions about attention. This video moves slowly through the narration with these materials held on the screen so that viewers might take them in. Listening to narration and processing the textual images on screen, however, requires the "reader" to process two informational channels at once. This challenge is complicated by the addition of the poem which scrolls down the screen. Ultimately, these attention issues bring us back to the forms in which scholarship can be produced. In text, we have no problem, pausing or reading backwards to take in materials more slowly. In time-based media, we have the same ability, but perhaps not the same expectations.

The piece also contains a number of video clips that are used to provide the visual elements of the piece. The imagery of wild horses, plays a smaller role in this video than in an earlier an earlier iteration, where it was primary (https://youtu.be/2dp29ZkZ-J4). The imagery of early drag racing is taken from multiple YouTube videos. Those images have been blended and mixed together with varying opacities to create a composite with multiple layers. These materials are transformed when translated to multiple surfaces, including a reflection on the guitar surface and projection on the narrator's body.

The text of the poem, "A Blessing" is included as part of the academic discussion of the work. The poem is short and with very few exceptions, scholarship that takes up the work reproduces the text in its discussion. As with print essays discussing the poem for scholarly and educatiobnal purposes, the video response reproduces the work. In this instance, the poem is also transformed as it is projecton onto surfaces and mixed with the imagery and sonics of the piece.

The remaining materials also primarily created by the author Cell phone footage provides a few of the author's yard scene from an upstairs window. Cell phone footage also captures the view out from the guitar, revealing a projector. Webcam footage shows the sung response and the narrator playing guitar, which have been created by the author.

About the Video

Suspicious reading inscribes itself in the psyche as . . . a mind-set equipped with distinct qualities: distance rather than closeness; guardedness rather than openness; aggression rather than submission; superiority rather than reverence; attentiveness rather than distraction; exposure rather than tact. -- Rita Felski, Suspicious Minds

This video opens with number of screens that show citations from articles that look at problems with methods of scholarly criticism. It then transitions into a critical response to the poem, "A Blessing," by James Wright. The narration and response to the poem were first performed at the 2016 Computers and Writing Conference.

The background screens in the opening segments of the video consists of captured images of typed citations taken from scholars writing about the limits and consequences of overly negative academic critique. "A hermeneutics of suspicion dissipates its problem-solving powers and loses much of its allure," Rita Felski tells us. Judth Butler and Timothy Bewes advocate an alternative approach that counters suspicion. Bewes calls it "generous," and notes how such readings can be emdodied and resist closure: "A generous reading is always, in part, a reading of ourselves reading. The thought it produces is never transferable, recognizable, paraphrasable, applicable, expoundable"

The response, in the version included here was a remake of an earlier effort that used horse imagery rather than the motif of drag racing. The switch from horse to racing imagery represents a move from the concrete to the abstract. Wright's poem deals directly with horses. The video that drifts further away from the direct link gains complexity through extra distance in its layers of meaning. In many ways this move increases the ambiguity of the video. Following Madeline Sorapure, who compares metaphor to metonomy, suggesting, "metaphor exploits similarity and substitution, while metonymy exploits contiguity and association," we might imagine a continuum from concrete to abstract imagery that can be deployed in screen composing. Choosing materials becomes a tuning process for balancing allusions and distance along the continuum, with the results transforming into texts that might open or close avenues for response.

James Wright's poem is a fine candidate for these open-ended approaches. Scholars have noted the epiphany that closes the poem, a move built upon conjecture over companionship. The poem brings together material boundaries with imagery of natural communion: "the horses are enclosed in 'barbed wire'; the poet and his friend must transgress an unnatural boundary to enter into the natural setting" (Pink 44). All of this crossing evokes a sense of moving beyond boundaries that provides the poet with an out-of-body experience. The experience of mixing and of moving beyond the body is embodied in the closing of the poem: "Suddenly I realize / That if I stepped out of my body I would break / Into blossom."

Neither the knower nor the known can be situated in advance of the occasion’s coming to be—both are immanent to the field’s composition. Nor can the knower- known relation be classified independently of the ecologies that compose it, including those whose register is unquantifiable, such as the quality or affective tonality of experience. -- Thought in Action 30

The video replicates this embodiment in its own closing scene. The choice to make the primary surface of the piece the shirt of the narrator offers a literal placement of media on the body. This move is amplified by the high levels of affect conveyed by the visual materials. Steven Shaviro argues that digital videos "like other media works, are machines for generating affect, and for capitalizing upon, or extracting value from, this affect" (2-3). Felski points out that affect offers an untapped resource for scholarship that might inspire or evoke empassioned responses. Projected on the body, emotions light in the body as insights or feelings, what Roland Barthes and Gregory Ulmer have discussed as the puunctum. Sarah Arroyo explains, "The concept of the punctum . . . provides access to knowledge residing in the body" (57). It is the sting, the wound, the spark, the mysterious recognition that registers as emotions.

Ambiguity flows through these emotional moments and materials. Composing in these registers yields a scholarship more open to interpretation. Pushing the focus of the compostion to creative production adds more ambiguity. In many ways the most open scholarship begins at the 05:00 mark in the video, with the images projected on the body and the sung response. The reading of the poem that it offers both contains and casts forth its secrets.
