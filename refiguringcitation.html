Refiguring Citation

This video was created for the 2016 Modern Language Convention, where it was presented with the narration performed as part of a panel on new practices for citation. It includes a number of student-created videos in which students reflect on their use of materials in multimedia projects. It also includes quotations from scholarly articles. It also includes materials from YouTube including captures of web screens and excerpts from a video on fair use practices.

Use of Materials

The bulk of this video is made up of student projects. These videos use screen recording to capture materials associated with class projects. They also include reflections by the students on aspects of learning. These particular videos focus on the use of materials in the student projects. They also contain citations of the items that students have incorporated into their work. The works that students reference are video poems composed for an undergraduate course. In the class, students agree to post drafts and revisions of video projects publicly. (Re)figuring Citation opens with an example of a video poem that has had its content blocked by YouTube. A video produced by YouTube called YouTube Copyright School is also excerpted at 12:48 in (Re)figuring Citation.

The video also contains screen recordings of web sites. Some sites have been integrated into (Re)figuring Citation--e.g., a discussion forum posting. Other sites appear in the student-created videos as part of reflections on the use of materials. Student videos also include materials captured from the web that are integrated into student video poems. These materials are cited and acknowledge in the reflection videos.

The video also includes images scanned from the MLA Handbook for Writers of Research Papers. The images were scanned with permission from a pre-publication draft of the book. At 01:22 in the video, a scanned image from the book shows a series of questions meant to guide students through flexible engagements with citation: Who is the author of the source? What is the title of the source? How was the source published? Where did you find the source? When was the source published? At 01:30, a final question is added: Why did you decide to use the source? At 5:36 in the video, a scanned page from the book appears with a passage that offers the metaphor of a container for understanding citation formats.

Key elements of the video are textual callouts or on screen typing with quotations from articles related to citation. Excerpts point to decision making and thinking associated with citation practices. Quotations also take up shifts in thinking and practices around digital composing. These are cited on screen in the video.

About the Video

Students patchwrite because they are engaging shallowly with their sources, usually working only with sentences gleaned from the first pages of the source. In a sense, they don't understand any of their sources. How could they, when they're not actually reading them? And when they don't understand--when they are merely quote-mining their sources--they patchwrite. (75)

Citation creates a continuum when it comes to the way we weave materials into projects. On one end, citations are evidential markers and links to sources--works cited. On another end, they are the stuff of thought, as composers bring together materials and mix them into projects. And this continuum is multi-channel, with legal, ethical, compositional, cultural concerns--ranges and questions--layered over one another as we work with sources. Screen composing adds more layers, for audiovisuals, for motion, for genres, for creative production.

Citation reveals how screen composing extends from and pushes against behaviors built on print mind sets. The norms that govern the use of materials for print, "we all take for granted," Lawrence Lessig, explains. How those norms apply to media is an open question, with one possible response resulting in "the norms of 'quote freely, with attribution' spread from text to music and film" (55). Lessig offers a legal or ethical frame for tracing the use-boundaries between print and media. A practice like remix recasts acceptable uses like "the right to quote" (55). Behaviors based on print meet in screen composing an active testing new modes of thinking and writing that use borrowing, loops, remix to bring together images, sounds and words. Print protocols and logics warn of the surface-level attention linked with quickly mixed citations. Screen composing responds by making quick mixing a fundamental inventive move.

The response offered by screen composing extends the patching together of materials to inlcude images, words, and sounds. These kinds of shifts and their associated disruptions are not new. Giles Deleuze traces similar disruptions tied to the integratrion of sound into cinema. Citing Godard, he notes that adding sound does not create a new, separate layer to accompany image; rather it alters the nature of images themselves as "mxing ousts montage, it being understood that mixing does not just consist of a distribution of the different sound elements, but the allocation of their differential relations with the visual elements" (175).

When digital writing practices ask (and often require) authors to experiment with the different kinds of author/ity that emerge when hybridizing and modifying genres, then an attendant ethos also needs to emerge. As such, the authorship category of digital craft criticism begins the project of (re) imagining the ethos imaginative writers occupy when appropriating, employing, or modifying language, ready-made texts, and/or particular genres. -- Digital Craft 392

Remixing in multimedia is complicated because not only are images themselves amalgams of visual, sonic, verbal, and time-based activity, they also must be understood in terms of the interstices between the items. "It is not a matter of following a chain of images, even across voids, but of getting out of the chain or the association" (174). In terms of decision making related to materials, this cinematic modality calls for weighing the ways items extend and complicate one another, or more precisely, create boundary spaces where this shaping plays out. As Deleuze puts it, "the interaction of two images engenders or traces a frontier which belongs to neither one nor the other" (175). With screen composing, like the cinema Deleuze imagines, even a citation as simple as integrating a snippet of sound into a video becomes layered and mixed with boundaries as a circuit of movement among materials emerges in "a network of sound communication and permutation, bearing noises, sounds, reflexive or interactive speech-acts and music" (228).

Digital craft criticism, Adam Koehler suggests, requires imagining a new ethos from which authors might experiment with such emerging modes. This ethos might deploy the kinds of approaches that Deleuze links with "cinema [that] is going to become an analytic of the image, implying a new conception of cutting, a whole 'pedagogy' which will operate in different ways" (22) Koehler's craft criticism "foregrounds textual production while backgrounding textual interpretation" (381). This production calls for thinking not only about but also through the the mediated materials of composing. The screen itself calls for the "camera-consciousness" that, Delueze suggests, "would no longer be defined by the movements [the camera] is able to follow or make, but by the mental connections it is able to enter into" (22). This consciousness emerges in the spaces of conjunction, the interstices where we find "questioning, responding, objecting, provoking, theorematizing, hypothesizing, experimenting" (22). How do these engagements relate to the mixing of materials into media projects? In terms of citation practices, the student reflective videos in (Re)figuring Citation perhaps offer examples of composers documenting the material decision making associated associated with these frontier spaces of screen composing.
